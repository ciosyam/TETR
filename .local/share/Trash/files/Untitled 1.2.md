_**Atropa belladonna**_, commonly known as **belladonna** or **deadly nightshade**, is a toxic [perennial]( "Perennial plant") [herbaceous plant]( "Herbaceous plant") in the nightshade family [Solanaceae]( "Solanaceae"),[[2]]( which also includes [tomatoes]( "Tomato"), [potatoes]( "Potato") and [aubergine]( "Aubergine") (Eggplant). It is native to [Europe]( "Europe") and [Western Asia]( "Western Asia"), including [Turkey]( "Turkey"). Its distribution extends from [Ireland]( "Ireland") in the west to western [Ukraine]( "Ukraine") and the [Iranian]( "Iran") province of [Gilan]( "Gilan") in the east. It is also naturalised or introduced in some parts of Canada, [North Africa]( "North Africa") and the United States.

The foliage and [berries]( "Berry (botany)") are extremely [toxic]( "Toxin") when ingested, containing [tropane alkaloids]( "Tropane alkaloid").[[1]]([[2]]([[3]]([[4]]( These toxins include [atropine]( "Atropine"), [scopolamine]( "Scopolamine"), and [hyoscyamine]( "Hyoscyamine"), which cause [delirium]( "Delirium") and [hallucinations]( "Hallucination"),[[1]]([[2]]([[3]]([[5]]([[6]]( and are also used as pharmaceutical [anticholinergics]( "Anticholinergic").[[1]]( Tropane alkaloids are of common occurrence not only in the [Old World]( "Old World") tribes [Hyoscyameae]( "Hyoscyameae") (to which the genus Atropa belongs) and [Mandragoreae]( "Mandragoreae"), but also in the [New World]( "New World") tribe [Datureae]( "Datureae") - all of which belong to the subfamily [Solanoideae]( "Solanoideae") of the plant family Solanaceae.[[1]](

_Atropa belladonna_ has unpredictable effects.[[2]]( The antidote for belladonna poisoning is [physostigmine]( "Physostigmine") or [pilocarpine]( "Pilocarpine"), the same as for [atropine]( "Atropine").[[7]](

## History[[edit]( "Edit section: History")]

_Atropa belladonna_ has a long history of use as a medicine, cosmetic, and poison.[[8]]([[3]]([[9]]( Known originally under various folk names (such as "deadly nightshade" in English), the plant was named _Atropa belladonna_ by [Carl Linnaeus]( "Carl Linnaeus") (1707–1778) when he devised his classification system. Linnaeus chose the genus name _Atropa_ because of the poisonous properties of these plants. [Atropos]( "Atropos") (lit. "unturning one"), one of the [Three Fates]( "Moirai") in Greek mythology, is said to have cut a person's thread of life after her sisters had spun and measured it. Linnaeus chose the species name _belladonna_ ("beautiful woman" in Italian) in reference to the cosmetic use of the plant during the [Renaissance]( "Renaissance"), when women used the juice of the berries in eyedrops intended to [dilate the pupils]( "Pupillary response") and make the eyes appear more seductive.[[10]]([[3]]([[11]]([[12]](

Extracts of [plants in the deadly nightshade family]( "Solanaceae") have been in use since at least the 4th century BC, when _Mandragora_ ([mandrake]( "Mandrake")) was recommended by [Theophrastus]( "Theophrastus") for treatment of wounds, gout, and sleeplessness, and as a love [potion]( "Potion"). In the first century BC, [Cleopatra]( "Cleopatra") used Atropine-rich extracts from the Egyptian [henbane]( "Henbane") plant (another nightshade) for the above-mentioned purpose of dilating the [pupils]( "Pupils") of her eyes.[_[citation needed]( "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The use of deadly nightshades as a poison was known in ancient Rome, as attested by the rumour that the Roman empress [Livia Drusilla]( "Livia") used the juice of _Atropa belladonna_ berries to murder her husband, the emperor [Augustus]( "Augustus").[[13]](

In the first century AD, [Dioscorides]( "Dioscorides") recognized wine of mandrake as an [anaesthetic]( "Anaesthetic") for treatment of pain or sleeplessness, to be given prior to surgery or cautery.[[14]]( The use of nightshade preparations for anaesthesia, often in combination with [opium]( "Opium"), persisted throughout the Roman and Islamic empires and continued in Europe until superseded in the 19th century by modern anaesthetics.

The modern pharmacological study of _Atropa belladonna_ extracts was begun by the [German]( "Germany") [chemist]( "Chemist") [Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge]( "Friedlieb Ferdinand Runge") (1795–1867). In 1831, the German pharmacist Heinrich F. G. Mein (1799–1864)[[15]]( succeeded in preparing a pure crystalline form of the active substance, named _atropine_.[[16]]( [[17]](

## Description[[edit]( "Edit section: Description")]

[![](](

_Atropa belladonna_

_Atropa belladonna_ is a branching [herbaceous]( "Herbaceous") perennial [rhizomatous]( "Rhizomatous") [hemicryptophyte]( "Hemicryptophyte"), often growing as a [subshrub]( "Subshrub") from a fleshy rootstock. Plants can reach a height of 2 m (7 ft) (more commonly 1.5 m (5 ft)), and have ovate leaves up to 18 cm (7 in) long. The bell-shaped [flowers]( "Flower") are dull purple tinged yellow-green toward the base and are faintly scented. The [fruits]( "Fruit") are [berries]( "Berry (botany)"), which are green, ripening to a shiny black, and approximately 1.5 cm (0.6 in) in diameter. The berries are sweet and are consumed by animals that disperse the [seeds]( "Seed") in their droppings, even though they contain toxic [alkaloids]( "Alkaloid") (see _[Toxicity]([[18]]( There is a pale-yellow flowering form called _Atropa belladonna_ var. _lutea_ with pale yellow fruit.

_A. belladonna_ is sometimes confused with the much less poisonous [black nightshade]( "Black nightshade"), _Solanum nigrum_, belonging to a different genus within [Solanaceae]( "Solanaceae").[[19]]( A comparison of the fruit shows that black nightshade berries are spherical, have a dull lustre and grow in clusters, whereas the berries of deadly nightshade are much glossier, twice as large, somewhat flattened and are borne singly. Another distinction is that black nightshade flowers are not tubular but white and star-shaped, bearing a central cone of yellow anthers.

## Distribution[[edit]( "Edit section: Distribution")]

_Atropa belladonna_ is native to temperate southern, Central and Eastern Europe, [Turkey]( "Turkey"), [Iran]( "Iran") and the [Caucasus]( "Caucasus"), but has been cultivated and introduced outside its native range. In southern Sweden it was recorded in Flora of Skåne in 1870 as grown in [apothecary]( "Apothecary") gardens near Malmö.[[20]](

In Britain it is native only on calcareous soils, on disturbed ground, field margins, hedgerows and open woodland. More widespread as an alien, it is often a relic of cultivation as a medicinal herb. Seed is spread mainly by birds.[[21]](

It is [naturalised]( "Naturalisation (biology)") in parts of [North America]( "North America") and [North Africa]( "North Africa"), where it is often found in shady, moist locations with [limestone]( "Limestone")-rich [soils]( "Soil"). It is considered a [weed]( "Weed") species in parts of the world,[[22]]( where it colonizes areas with disturbed soils.[[23]](

## Cultivation[[edit]( "Edit section: Cultivation")]

[![](](

Belladonna cultivation, Eli Lilly and Company, 1919

_Atropa belladonna_ is rarely used in gardens, but, when grown, it is usually for its large upright [habit]( "Habit (biology)") and showy berries.[[24]]( [Germination]( "Germination") of the small seeds is often difficult, due to hard seed coats that cause seed [dormancy]( "Dormancy"). Germination takes several weeks under alternating temperature conditions, but can be sped up with the use of [gibberellic acid]( "Gibberellic acid").[[25]]( The seedlings need [sterile]( "Sterilization (microbiology)") [soil]( "Soil") to prevent [damping off]( "Damping off") and resent root disturbance during transplanting.[_[citation needed]( "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

## Taxonomy[[edit]( "Edit section: Taxonomy")]

_Atropa belladonna_ is in the [nightshade]( "Solanum") family (_[Solanaceae]( "Solanaceae")_), which it shares with [potatoes]( "Potato"), [tomatoes]( "Tomato"), [eggplants]( "Eggplant"), [jimsonweed]( "Jimsonweed"), [tobacco]( "Tobacco"), [wolfberry]( "Wolfberry"), and [chili peppers]( "Chili pepper"). The common names for this species include belladonna, deadly nightshade, divale, dwale,[[26]]( banewort, devil's berries, death cherries, beautiful death, devil's herb, great morel, and dwayberry.[[27]](

## Etymology[[edit]( "Edit section: Etymology")]

The name _Atropa belladonna_ was published by [Carl Linnaeus]( "Carl Linnaeus") in _[Species Plantarum]( "Species Plantarum")_ in 1753.[[28]]( _Atropa_ is derived from the name of the Greek goddess [Atropos]( "Atropos") ('she who may not be turned aside' i.e. 'the inflexible' or 'the implacable')—one of the three [Greek fates or destinies]( "Moirai") who would determine the course of a man's life by the weaving of threads that symbolized his birth, the events in his life, and finally his death, with Atropos cutting these threads to mark the last of these.[[29]]([[30]]( The name "belladonna" comes from the two words _bella_ and _donna_ in the [Italian language]( "Italian language"), meaning 'beautiful' and 'woman', respectively,[[26]]( originating either from its usage as a cosmetic to beautify pallid skin,[[31]]( or more probably, from its usage to increase the pupil size in women.[[29]]([[30]](

## Toxicity[[edit]( "Edit section: Toxicity")]

[![](](

Attractively sweet and cherry-like fruit of _Atropa belladonna_

Belladonna is one of the most [toxic]( "Toxicity") plants known,[[32]]([[33]]( and its use by mouth increases risk in numerous clinical conditions, such as [complications of pregnancy]( "Complications of pregnancy"), [cardiovascular diseases]( "Cardiovascular disease"), [gastrointestinal disorders]( "Gastrointestinal disorders"), and [psychiatric disorders]( "Psychiatric disorders"), among others.[[2]]([[3]]( All parts of the plant contain [tropane alkaloids]( "Tropane alkaloids");[[2]]([[34]]([[35]]( roots have up to 1.3%,[[a]]( leaves 1.2%, stalks 0.65%, flowers 0.6%, ripe berries 0.7%, and seeds 0.4% tropane alkaloids; leaves reach maximal alkaloid content when the plant is budding and flowering, roots are most poisonous in the end of the plant's vegetation period.[[36]]( Belladonna nectar is transformed by bees into honey that also contains tropane alkaloids.[[37]]( The berries pose the greatest danger to children because they look attractive and have a somewhat sweet taste.[[27]]([[38]]([[39]]( The root of the plant is generally the most toxic part, though this can vary from one specimen to another.[[27]]([[34]](

The active agents in belladonna, [atropine]( "Atropine"), [hyoscine]( "Hyoscine") (scopolamine), and [hyoscyamine]( "Hyoscyamine"), have [anticholinergic]( "Anticholinergic") properties.[[3]]([[40]]([[35]]( The symptoms of belladonna [poisoning]( "Poison") include [dilated]( "Mydriasis") [pupils]( "Pupil"), sensitivity to light, blurred [vision]( "Visual perception"), [tachycardia]( "Tachycardia"), loss of [balance]( "Sense of balance"), staggering, headache, [rash]( "Rash"), flushing, severely dry mouth and throat, slurred speech, [urinary retention]( "Urinary retention"), [constipation]( "Constipation"), [confusion]( "Mental confusion"), [hallucinations]( "Hallucination"), delirium, and convulsions.[[3]]([[32]]([[41]]([[42]]([[35]]( In 2009, _A. belladonna_ berries were mistaken for [blueberries]( "Blueberries") by an adult woman; the six berries she ate were documented to result in severe [anticholinergic syndrome]( "Anticholinergic syndrome").[[43]]( The plant's deadly symptoms are caused by atropine's disruption of the [parasympathetic nervous system]( "Parasympathetic nervous system")'s ability to regulate involuntary activities, such as sweating, breathing, and heart rate. The [antidote]( "Antidote") for belladonna poisoning is an [anticholinesterase]( "Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor") (such as [physostigmine]( "Physostigmine")) or a [cholinomimetic]( "Cholinomimetic") (such as [pilocarpine]( "Pilocarpine")), the same as for atropine.[[44]](
